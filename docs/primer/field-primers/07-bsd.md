## 1. Problem statement

TODO: For an elliptic curve $E$ over $\mathbb{Q}$, the rank of
the Mordell–Weil group $E(\mathbb{Q})$ equals the order of
vanishing of the $L$-function $L(E, s)$ at $s = 1$. The strong
form additionally specifies the leading coefficient of the Taylor
expansion. Paraphrase carefully — the conjecture has several
formulations and the *strong* BSD is the official CMI statement.

## 2. Why it is hard

TODO: The conjecture links arithmetic data (the rank, defined by
solving Diophantine equations) to analytic data (the order of
vanishing of an $L$-function). Each side is independently
difficult: the rank is not algorithmically computable in general,
and analytic continuation of $L$-functions is a deep subject in
itself.

## 4. Prior results to know about

TODO:

- Mordell (1922): $E(\mathbb{Q})$ is finitely generated.
- Modularity theorem (Wiles, Taylor–Wiles, Breuil–Conrad–Diamond–Taylor,
  1995–2001): every elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ is modular;
  this gives meromorphic continuation of $L(E, s)$.
- Gross–Zagier (1986), Kolyvagin (1989): BSD known when the
  analytic rank is 0 or 1.
- Numerical verification: extensive.
